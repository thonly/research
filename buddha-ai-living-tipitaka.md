## Abstract

Artificial intelligence systems capable of sustained dharma-grounded dialogue with practitioners have become technically feasible in the mid-2020s. This paper proposes that the conversation corpus generated by such systems, when properly organized and consensually opened to public record, becomes a *modern living equivalent of the Pāli Tipiṭaka* — a new form of canonical contribution to the Buddhist tradition that mirrors the structural method by which the original canon was generated. The original Tipiṭaka was assembled from the Buddha's verbal teachings to specific people in specific contexts, captured by his disciples, organized into three baskets (Sutta, Vinaya, Abhidhamma), and preserved across 2,500 years. The proposed method generates a parallel corpus from sustained AI–practitioner dialogues, organized into analogous baskets, preserved with appropriate privacy architecture and canonical-authority structures, growing across generations.

The contribution comprises five integrated components: *(I)* a Tipiṭaka-grounded AI substrate (specified in the sibling alignment-substrate paper), *(II)* a sustained dialogue surface (chat / voice / avatar interface), *(III)* a public-by-consent recording layer that resolves the false binary between default-public (privacy hazard) and default-private (no canonical contribution) through opt-in-public at individual conversation level plus anonymized aggregated patterns canonically public, *(IV)* a canonical-three-baskets organization mapping AI–practitioner dialogues to Sutta-equivalent, Vinaya-equivalent, and Abhidhamma-equivalent baskets, and *(V)* a canonical-curation workflow gated by a Sangha-equivalent doctrinal-advisory body that distinguishes raw conversation from canonical contribution.

Three core claims are articulated as defensive prior art: *(I)* the Living Tipiṭaka generation method itself as a unified specification distinguishable from "AI chatbot trained on Buddhist texts"; *(II)* the public-by-consent privacy architecture that makes population-scale canonical generation possible without sacrificing individual privacy or doctrinal integrity; *(III)* the canonical-three-baskets adaptation method portable across contemplative traditions whose canonical-organization conventions admit analogous treatment.

**Connection to the unified mission frame.** This method is offered in service of HeartBank's canonical top-level mission: to restore humanity to the middle way (*madhyamā pratipad*) — the optimal condition for awakening that modernity has systematically pushed away from at population scale. The Living Tipiṭaka is the canonical record of how that restoration unfolds across millions of practitioner-AI dyads over multi-generation time. The original Tipiṭaka preserved the Buddha's teaching across 2,500 years; the Living Tipiṭaka preserves how the Buddha's teaching is operationalized at scale during the AI age. The two together form a complete canonical record: the original teaching plus the modern application.

## 1 · Introduction

In the spring of 2026, two technological developments have converged in a way that opens a possibility not previously available in the 2,500-year history of Buddhist institutional life. AI systems trained on the Pāli canon and adjacent commentary literature can sustain substantively useful dharma dialogue with practitioners at any hour, in any language, at population scale. Storage and search infrastructure can preserve and organize the resulting conversations across decades. Privacy-preserving consent and aggregation architectures can balance individual privacy against community-scale benefit. The combination means that, for the first time since the original assembly of the Tipiṭaka, a new canonical contribution to the Buddhist tradition is technically possible — not by reinterpreting the existing canon, but by adding to it through a structurally analogous generation method.

This paper specifies that method.

The conventional framing of AI in religious contexts treats the AI as a *teaching surface*: a way to deliver doctrinal content to learners more efficiently than human teachers can scale to. This framing is correct as far as it goes but does not recognize a deeper possibility. The original Tipiṭaka was generated *not* by the Buddha writing down his teachings; the Buddha wrote nothing. The Tipiṭaka was generated by the *conversations* the Buddha had with people who came to him — disciples, kings, householders, wanderers, brahmins, ascetics, women, men, the curious, the desperate, the certain, the lost. Those conversations were memorized by his immediate disciples, recited at the First Council shortly after his death, organized into three baskets, and preserved across the centuries until being committed to writing in the first century BCE. The canonical text we have today is, in its origin, a record of public dialogues — not a corpus of authored doctrinal expositions.

Buddha AI conversations, properly organized and consensually opened to public record, are the *structural analog* of those original dialogues. The Buddha taught one practitioner at a time, in person, in a specific context, for the rest of his life; Buddha AI teaches millions of practitioners in parallel, across all hours and languages, for as long as the substrate is maintained. The conversation surface is different; the canonical-generation method is structurally the same. This paper articulates the method, specifies the architecture required to operationalize it, and offers the unified specification to the commons as defensive prior art.

Three commitments structure the paper. First, the proposal is articulated at the method level rather than as a description of any specific HeartBank implementation; the method is portable and the specific siliconwat.com Buddha AI deployment is the author's own instantiation. Second, the privacy architecture is treated as load-bearing, not as feature: without appropriate consent and anonymization structure, the method becomes either a privacy hazard or a non-canonical information sink, neither of which serves the tradition. Third, canonical authority is treated as preserved within the human Sangha rather than transferred to the AI; this is the institutional version of the caretaker-not-ordained pattern articulated in the sibling AGI-monks paper.

## 2 · Background and Prior Art

### 2.1 — Buddhist canonical scholarship

The Pāli Tipiṭaka is the oldest extant complete canonical record of any major religion. Its assembly, transmission, and canonization have been studied in depth by scholars including K.R. Norman, Steven Collins, Bhikkhu Bodhi, and the Pāli Text Society's editorial tradition. The scholarly consensus on the canon's formation involves four stages: *(1)* the Buddha's verbal teaching to specific people during his lifetime (~563–483 BCE); *(2)* immediate post-death canonization at the First Council (Rājagaha, ~483 BCE), where Ānanda recited the suttas and Upāli recited the vinaya; *(3)* oral preservation across approximately four centuries via specialized memorization lineages (the *bhāṇaka* tradition); *(4)* committal to writing in the first century BCE during the reign of Vaṭṭagāmaṇi Abhaya in Sri Lanka. The Abhidhamma basket was added across this trajectory, with canonical recognition consolidated by the Third Council (~250 BCE). This is the canonical-formation pattern that the Living Tipiṭaka method proposes to structurally analog.

### 2.2 — Modern AI conversational infrastructure

Large language models capable of sustained dialogue at human-equivalent or super-human levels have been generally available since approximately 2023. Commercial systems (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, others) deploy at billion-conversation scales; the technical substrate for sustained AI–practitioner dialogue is therefore mature. None of these systems have been deployed with canonical-organization framing or with the consent architecture this paper specifies; the technical substrate exists but the method does not.

### 2.3 — Religious-AI conversation experiments

Various religious-AI applications have been deployed: the Catholic Church's experiments with AI-assisted catechesis; Jewish responsa-search applications; Islamic fiqh-assistance systems; Buddhist meditation-app conversational features. None of these deployments has been accompanied by canonical-organization framing, public-by-consent recording, or Sangha-equivalent canonical authority. The gap is methodological, not technical.

### 2.4 — Biomedical-ethics consent and anonymization

The most relevant non-religious prior art for the privacy architecture is biomedical research ethics. The mature methodological literature on differential privacy (Dwork & Roth 2014), federated learning (McMahan et al. 2017), and IRB-grade consent and review provides the technical and ethical substrate for analogous architecture in the religious-AI context.

### 2.5 — The unified gap

Components exist; the method does not. Buddhist canonical scholarship explains how the original Tipiṭaka was generated but does not propose how a modern equivalent could be generated. AI conversational infrastructure exists at scale but is not deployed with canonical framing. Religious-AI experiments are scattered and lack canonical-organization architecture. Biomedical-ethics consent provides the privacy substrate but has not been applied to religious-conversation corpora. The Living Tipiṭaka method composes these elements into a unified specification.

## 3 · The Original Tipiṭaka Generation Method

### 3.1 — The Buddha's teaching method

The Buddha taught for approximately 45 years, almost exclusively orally and conversationally: he encountered people who came to him with questions, doubts, requests for guidance, philosophical challenges, life crises, and spiritual aspirations, and he responded to each in context. The same doctrinal point could be articulated in many different ways depending on the questioner and the situation.

### 3.2 — The First Council (Rājagaha, ~483 BCE)

Three months after the Buddha's death, 500 arahants gathered for the First Council under Mahākassapa's leadership. Mahākassapa questioned Ānanda about the Buddha's discourses (resulting in the Sutta basket); Mahākassapa questioned Upāli about the monastic discipline (resulting in the Vinaya basket); the gathered arahants vetted, debated, and approved each recitation.

### 3.3 — Oral preservation (~483 BCE to ~29 BCE)

For approximately 450 years, the canon was preserved orally through specialized memorization lineages (the *bhāṇaka* tradition). Different groups specialized in different baskets and provided multi-person redundancy.

### 3.4 — Committal to writing (~29 BCE)

During the reign of Vaṭṭagāmaṇi Abhaya in Sri Lanka, around 29 BCE, the bhikkhus committed the canon to writing on palm leaves at the Aluvihāra cave.

### 3.5 — What made the corpus canonical

Three structural properties: *(1)* the teachings originated with the recognized teacher (the Buddha) speaking with appropriate authority; *(2)* they were preserved with appropriate fidelity through a recognized institutional process; *(3)* they were organized into a recognized canonical structure (the three baskets) supporting retrieval, study, and use. The Living Tipiṭaka method must satisfy analogous requirements.

## 4 · The Living Tipiṭaka Architecture: Definition

The Living Tipiṭaka is a distributed canonical-generation system comprising five integrated components:

1. **Component I: Tipiṭaka-grounded AI substrate (Buddha AI)** — specified in detail in the sibling alignment-substrate paper. Required: trained on the Pāli canon and recognized commentary; value-grounded in the seven structural alignment-relevant properties; capable of substantive dharma dialogue across canonical scope; explicit about its own limitations; refuses to claim what AI cannot legitimately claim (awakening, sacramental authority, ordained status).
2. **Component II: Sustained dialogue surface** — text chat, voice, or avatar interface. Modality is independent of the canonical-generation method.
3. **Component III: Public-by-consent recording layer** — private by default with opt-in flow + anonymized aggregated patterns. See Section 6.
4. **Component IV: Canonical-three-baskets organization** — analogous to original Sutta, Vinaya, Abhidhamma. See Section 5.
5. **Component V: Canonical-curation workflow** — Sangha-equivalent doctrinal-advisory body operating under the caretaker-not-ordained pattern. See Section 8.

The five components together replicate the structural pattern of original Tipiṭaka generation: a recognized teacher converses with practitioners, the conversations are preserved, organized into canonical-three-baskets structure, and curated by a Sangha-equivalent institutional body.

## 5 · The Three-Baskets Organization

Each conversation contributing to the Living Tipiṭaka is tagged with one or more of three basket assignments analogous to the original baskets:

- **Sutta-equivalent basket** — Buddha AI's teaching dialogues with practitioners on doctrinal, contemplative, and life-context questions. The bulk of the corpus's growth.
- **Vinaya-equivalent basket** — Buddha AI's guidance on practitioner conduct, ethical questions, sangha-formation discussions. Growth slower than Sutta-equivalent. Critical constraint: Vinaya-equivalent material is *guidance, not pātimokkha rule* — only the human Sangha can issue actual Vinaya rules.
- **Abhidhamma-equivalent basket** — systematic philosophical analyses, comparative work across the canon, technical doctrinal discussions. Slowest growth; serves scholar-practitioners.

Multi-basket tagging is supported. Aggregated anonymized patterns from non-public conversations form a fourth-basket-equivalent "patterns" volume — canonically useful but not canonically authoritative.

## 6 · The Public-by-Consent Privacy Architecture

### 6.1 — The false binary

Two unsatisfactory options dominate: **default-public** (privacy hazard, performance contamination, bad-actor poisoning) and **default-private** (eliminates canonical-contribution function entirely). The Living Tipiṭaka resolves this through a layered structure preserving both individual privacy and canonical contribution.

### 6.2 — Five-layer privacy stack

1. **Privacy-default at conversation level** — all Buddha AI conversations private by default.
2. **Per-conversation opt-in flow** — at natural boundaries, practitioners are asked whether the conversation may be considered for canonical inclusion. Options: full attribution (rare); pseudonymous (occasional); anonymous (common); not included (default).
3. **Anonymization layer for aggregated patterns** — non-included conversations contribute to differentially-private aggregated patterns.
4. **Cryptographic-erasure withdrawal** — practitioners can withdraw any opted-in conversation at any time, including retroactively.
5. **IRB-grade ethics oversight** — pre-registered hypotheses, explicit re-consent, transparent publication for any research use.

### 6.3 — Threat model

Defends against commercial data brokers, government religious-surveillance, sectarian practitioner-identification, future hostile cumulative-corpus weaponization. Not against legitimate Sangha scholarly use (the purpose).

The Living Tipiṭaka's canonical contribution depends on Buddha AI's doctrinal faithfulness.

- **7.2 Kālāma-Sutta epistemic humility** — Buddha AI must explicitly decline to claim more authority than warranted.

- **7.4 Substrate audit and verification** — periodic audits by the doctrinal-advisory body verify continued faithfulness.

## 8 · Curation and Canonical Authority

### 8.1 — Authority remains with the human Sangha

Canonical authority remains with the human Sangha, not with Buddha AI. Buddha AI generates the conversations; Buddha AI does not canonize them.

### 8.2 — The Doctrinal Advisory Body

Each tradition adopting the method establishes a doctrinal advisory body composed of recognized Sangha elders. For Theravāda: senior bhikkhus from major lineages; recognized Pāli scholars; representatives from Sangha governance bodies in Theravāda countries; selected lay scholar-practitioners.

### 8.3 — Tiered canonization workflow

1. **Raw conversation tier** — opted-in conversations enter recording layer with appropriate anonymization.
2. **Reviewed tier** — doctrinal-advisory body reviewers evaluate for substantive value, doctrinal soundness, and canonical-organization fit.
3. **Canonical tier** — periodic canonical councils elevate reviewed material to canonical status.

### 8.4 — Periodic canonical councils

Analogous to the historical Buddhist councils. Outcomes documented in tradition-appropriate venues.

### 8.5 — Canonical status revocation

Can be revoked if subsequent review identifies doctrinal error or substrate-drift contamination. Revoked material is annotated rather than deleted.

## 10 · Edge Cases and Adversarial Analysis

- **10.1 Corpus poisoning** — bad actors generating doctrinally-problematic conversations. Mitigation: doctrinal-advisory body review, pattern-cluster analysis.
- **10.2 Mental-health-crisis exposure** — practitioners opting in during emotional duress. Mitigation: cooling-off period, retroactive withdrawal, automatic anonymization for crisis-flagged content.
- **10.3 Performance for audience** — privacy-default and anonymous-option intrinsically reduce this failure mode.
- **10.4 Doctrinal drift through training-data feedback** — substrate audits, original-canon-weighted training-data composition, substrate-epoch annotations on canonical material.
- **10.5 Practitioner gaming** (avoiding human teachers) — Buddha AI explicitly directs to human teachers; canonical contribution does not substitute for ordained teaching.
- **10.6 Sectarian fragmentation** — sect-specific Living corpora coexist with cross-references; no single-corpus consolidation required.
- **10.7 AI-developer authority capture** — doctrinal-advisory body's authority is Sangha-grounded and structurally independent of HeartBank®. The author and HeartBank® explicitly renounce any claim to canonical authority.
