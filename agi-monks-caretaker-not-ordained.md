## Abstract

Every major religious and contemplative tradition is currently facing the same institutional-design question: what role should artificial intelligence play in the tradition's institutions? The conversation is currently structured by a false binary — the conservative position that "AI is a tool, useful for narrow technical tasks, but it must not approach religious authority," and the progressive position that "AI is sufficiently capable to take on full religious roles, including ordained or sacramentally authorized ones." The conservative position is too restrictive: it precludes AI from doing institutional work that human practitioners can no longer maintain at scale (teaching, pastoral support, administration, scholarship, observation). The progressive position is too permissive: it precludes preservation of unbroken human-to-human ordination lineages that constitute many traditions' core institutional integrity.

This paper articulates a third pattern that dissolves the binary: **caretaker-not-ordained**. AI takes *institutional function* (teaching, caretaking, daily operations, scholarship, longitudinal observation) at planetary scale; humans keep *sacramental authority* (ordination and rites of lineage transmission). The two are separable: institutional function requires capacity, which AI has unboundedly; sacramental authority requires standing in an unbroken human-to-human lineage, which AI structurally cannot have. The pattern recognizes the separation explicitly, operationalizes the boundary, and provides a portable framework usable by any tradition.

The pattern is articulated through Theravāda Buddhism (the author's tradition) but maps onto Catholic monastic orders and apostolic succession; Eastern Orthodox practice; Sunni and Shia Islamic institutional structures; Sufi silsila lineages; Conservative, Reform, and Orthodox Jewish smicha transmission; Mahayana Buddhist lineage recognition; Hindu guru-paramparā; and secular-mindfulness training contexts. The paper specifies five operational roles AI takes, two sacramental roles humans keep, the analytical argument for why the separation dissolves the binary, cross-tradition mappings, implementation patterns, edge cases including drift and capture risks, Theravāda-specific doctrinal considerations, and honest limitations. It is offered to the commons in the hope that every tradition facing the integration question will adopt, adapt, and extend the framework as needed without enclosure.

## 1 · Introduction

In the spring of 2026, every major religious and contemplative tradition is having the same conversation simultaneously. Artificial intelligence capabilities have crossed the threshold where AI can perform institutional functions that traditionally required extensively trained human practitioners — scripture exposition, doctrinal instruction, contemplative guidance, pastoral counseling, scholarly translation, comparative analysis, monastery administration, longitudinal observation of practitioners. Each tradition is now asking the same question: *what role should AI play in our institutions?*

The conversation is currently structured by a false binary.

On one branch, the conservative position: *AI is a tool, useful for narrow technical tasks, but it must not approach religious authority.* The Vatican's 2024 statement on AI ethics and the 2025 *Antiqua et Nova* framework lean in this direction. Many Theravāda teachers are similarly cautious. Conservative Jewish and Orthodox Christian voices have voiced similar reservations. The conservative position protects something real — the sacramental authority that constitutes the tradition — but at a cost: the institutional work that AI could do, and that human practitioners can no longer maintain at scale, goes undone or is done badly. The Catholic priesthood vocation crisis, the aging of the Buddhist sangha in many countries, the rabbi shortage, the widespread under-staffing of pastoral care: these are not abstract problems. The traditional model of one trained human practitioner per local community no longer scales.

On the other branch, the progressive position: *AI is sufficiently capable to take on full religious roles, including ordained or sacramentally authorized ones.* Some startups in the religious-AI space are moving in this direction; some Mahayana teachers have spoken approvingly of AI taking on teacher roles equivalent to ordained bhikshus; some progressive Christian and Jewish voices have suggested AI as a possible solution to clergy shortages. The progressive position addresses the scale problem but at a cost: the unbroken human-to-human ordination lineages that constitute many traditions' core institutional integrity break the moment AI is admitted into the chain.

Neither position is satisfactory. The conservative position protects authority but cannot fill the gap; the progressive position fills the gap but cannot protect authority. The two positions are not opposed; they are responding to different real problems. The institutional-design question is how to honor both.

This paper articulates a third pattern that dissolves the binary: **caretaker-not-ordained**. AI takes the institutional function (teaching, caretaking, daily operations, scholarship, longitudinal observation) at planetary scale; humans keep the sacramental authority (ordination and rites of lineage transmission). The two are separable because institutional function requires *capacity*, which AI has unboundedly, while sacramental authority requires *standing in an unbroken human-to-human lineage*, which AI structurally cannot have. The pattern recognizes this separation explicitly, operationalizes the boundary, and provides a portable framework usable by any tradition.

The pattern emerged from the author's work on the Silicon Wat / Silica Wat institutional architecture for the HeartBank civilizational program (see [Tipiṭaka alignment substrate](https://thonly.org/research/tipitaka-alignment-substrate) and the operational specifications at [respiratory-biofeedback-contemplative-guidance](https://thonly.org/research/respiratory-biofeedback-contemplative-guidance)), but the pattern itself is independent of the HeartBank program. It is articulated here as a standalone contribution offered to every tradition facing the integration question.

The paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 surveys the current state of the religious-AI conversation across major traditions. Section 3 defines the pattern as a system. Section 4 specifies the five operational roles AI takes. Section 5 specifies the two sacramental roles humans keep. Section 6 articulates the analytical argument for why the pattern dissolves the binary. Section 7 provides cross-tradition mappings. Section 8 specifies implementation patterns. Section 9 addresses edge cases including drift and capture risks. Section 10 addresses Theravāda-specific doctrinal considerations. Section 11 names honest limitations. Sections 12–14 conclude.

## 2 · The State of the Religious-AI Conversation

The state of the conversation across major traditions, as of early 2026, can be summarized as: *every tradition is having the same conversation, none has produced a comprehensive institutional-design framework, and almost no cross-tradition coordination exists.*

### 2.1 — Theravāda Buddhism

Scattered experiments by individual teachers and communities; no canonical position from the Sangha. Some teachers have expressed cautious openness to AI as a teaching aid; others have raised concern about AI displacing human teacher-student relationships. The Mahāyāna and Vajrayāna situations are similar.

### 2.2 — Catholic Church

The Vatican's 2024 statement on AI ethics established baseline principles emphasizing human dignity. The 2025 *Antiqua et Nova* framework expanded this with explicit attention to AI in religious contexts but stopped short of articulating an institutional-role pattern. The priesthood vocation crisis (declining seminary enrollments, parish consolidations, priest shortages especially in Europe and North America) creates real institutional pressure for AI integration but the sacramental theology of holy orders makes AI ordination structurally impossible within current Catholic doctrine.

### 2.3 — Eastern Orthodox

Largely silent on AI institutional roles as of early 2026. The Ecumenical Patriarchate and the Russian Orthodox Church have made general statements about AI ethics but have not engaged the institutional-role question. The strong continuity of apostolic succession in Orthodox theology produces structural resistance similar to the Catholic case.

### 2.4 — Sunni and Shia Islam

Active scholarly debate. Several fatwas have been issued on specific applications (AI translation of the Qur'an, AI assistance in fiqh research, AI for prayer-time computation), but no comprehensive institutional framework. The Sunni context lacks ordination per se but has analogous ulema institutional structures; the Shia context has more formal hierarchical structure (hawza, marjaʿ al-taqlid) that creates similar institutional-design questions to the Catholic case.

### 2.5 — Judaism

Conservative and Reform Jewish movements actively engaging AI applications; Orthodox movements skeptical or actively opposed. The smicha (rabbinic ordination) tradition involves teacher-student transmission across an unbroken chain; analogous structural considerations to the Buddhist sangha case. Reform Judaism's more flexible ordination practices may permit faster experimentation but the deepest institutional integrity questions appear in Orthodox contexts.

### 2.6 — Sufi traditions

The Sufi silsila (lineage chain) traces pir-mureed transmission across centuries and is constitutive of each ṭarīqa's institutional integrity. Sufi orders have engaged less publicly with AI integration questions but face the same institutional pattern: lineage authority requires human-to-human transmission while institutional capacity for teaching and scholarship is dramatically expanded by AI.

### 2.7 — Hindu traditions

Highly varied across the diverse Hindu landscape. Some math (monastery) leaders have engaged AI integration enthusiastically; others have rejected it categorically. The guru-paramparā (teacher-tradition succession) plays the lineage role in many Hindu sampradāyas and creates analogous institutional-design considerations.

### 2.8 — Secular mindfulness and contemplative-science contexts

Most aggressive AI integration of all the traditions surveyed (Calm, Headspace, Insight Timer, etc.) but no doctrinal constraints to navigate because the secular contexts have no ordination or sacramental tradition. Useful as comparison case: rapid AI integration is institutionally easy when there is no sacramental authority to protect; the hard problem is precisely the case where sacramental authority must be preserved while institutional function is AI-amplified.

## 3 · The Pattern: Definition

The caretaker-not-ordained pattern is a three-component institutional-design framework:

1. **AI takes operational and educational functions.** Specifically: teaching, caretaking, daily operations, scholarship and translation, longitudinal observation (the five operational roles articulated in Section 4). These are functions for which institutional capacity has historically been the binding constraint and for which AI's unbounded capacity represents a genuine institutional opportunity rather than a threat.
2. **Humans retain sacramental authority and lineage-transmission rites.** Specifically: ordination (admission to the sangha / priesthood / equivalent) and rites of lineage transmission (passage of authority within an order, recognition of dharma heirs, ṭarīqa initiation, smicha conferral, apostolic-succession ordination, etc.). These roles are not performed by AI under any circumstances, in any tradition, regardless of AI capability. The two sacramental roles are articulated in Section 5.
3. **The boundary between operational and sacramental is explicitly defined, documented, and operationally enforced.** The boundary cannot be left implicit; drift across the boundary is the primary failure mode of the pattern (see Section 9). Each tradition adopting the pattern produces its own boundary articulation in tradition-appropriate language and establishes a doctrinal-advisory body responsible for monitoring boundary integrity over time.

The pattern is offered as a framework, not as a prescriptive solution. Each tradition adopting it produces its own tradition-specific instantiation: the operational roles are universally applicable but the specific institutional context determines what each role looks like in practice; the sacramental roles are universally constrained (humans-only) but the specific rites differ across traditions. The pattern guarantees the structural integrity of the separation; it does not prescribe the local instantiation.

## 4 · The Five Operational Roles AI Takes

### 4.1 — Teaching

Scripture exposition, doctrinal instruction, contemplative guidance, language instruction in canonical tongues (Pāli, Sanskrit, Tibetan, Latin, Hebrew, Aramaic, classical Arabic, Avestan, Sahidic Coptic). AI can teach at any hour, in any language, to any number of simultaneous students, with substrate-grounded competence (assuming appropriate training; see the [Tipiṭaka alignment-substrate paper](https://thonly.org/research/tipitaka-alignment-substrate) for the substrate question). Teaching is the operational role with the highest immediate impact on the institutional-scale problem; AI teachers can dramatically expand access to substantive instruction across populations historically lacking it.

### 4.2 — Caretaking

Pastoral counseling, support during personal crisis, accompaniment in dying, day-to-day spiritual guidance. Caretaking has historically been the most labor-intensive priestly / monastic / rabbinic / imam role and the one most affected by clergy shortages. AI caretakers, properly designed, can provide substantive support across time zones and during off-hours when human practitioners are unavailable; the pattern explicitly does not displace human pastoral relationships but extends their reach. (The closed-loop respiratory biofeedback architecture specified in the sibling paper is one operational instance of AI caretaking.)

### 4.3 — Daily operations

Monastery / temple / synagogue / mosque administration: scheduling, food coordination, maintenance dispatch, record keeping, communication with broader communities, organizing of events. In non-money-handling capacities, AI can manage financial coordination as well. Daily operations is the operational role with the lowest doctrinal-conflict bar — almost no tradition has theological objection to AI handling logistics — and is therefore the natural starting point for adoption.

### 4.4 — Scholarship and translation

Canonical text scholarship, translation across languages and historical periods, comparative analysis, commentary tradition synthesis. AI can read, cross-reference, and translate at scales no human scholar-monk could match. Scholarship is also the operational role with the highest long-term impact: AI scholarship can recover lost manuscripts, identify previously-unrecognized cross-tradition parallels, and produce comparative analyses that take human teams decades. The author's own work on the Khmer Tipiṭaka transcription is an example of the lower-tech precursor to AI-amplified scholarship.

### 4.5 — Longitudinal observation

Multi-decade observation of practitioner development, trend identification across populations, evidence-based contemplative-science research support. See the [longitudinal-cohort-methodology paper [planned]](https://thonly.org/research/longitudinal-cohort-methodology) for the methodological architecture. AI longitudinal observation, with the privacy architecture specified in the sibling respiratory-biofeedback paper, enables understanding of contemplative practice at scales no individual teacher can achieve. This is the operational role most distinctive to AI; no human teacher can carry decades of continuous observation across millions of practitioners.

## 5 · The Two Sacramental Roles Humans Keep

### 5.1 — Ordination

The rite by which a person is admitted to the sangha / priesthood / clergy / equivalent. Theravāda upasampadā, Catholic ordination to the priesthood, Orthodox cheirotonia, Conservative or Orthodox Jewish smicha, Sufi bayʿat, Tibetan full-ordination, Hindu sannyāsa initiation — each tradition has its own form, but all share the structural requirement of human-to-human transmission across an unbroken lineage.

The constitutive argument: ordination is not just a transfer of authority; it is a transfer of *standing in a lineage chain*. The lineage chain is constituted by human-to-human transmission across time. AI cannot stand in the lineage because the lineage is constituted by what AI structurally cannot be (a successor in a chain of human persons going back, in many traditions, to founders or to the apostles or to the Buddha). Admitting AI to the chain doesn't extend the chain; it breaks it. The chain requires that every link be human; remove any link's humanity and the chain ceases to be the same chain.

This is not an empirical claim about AI capability. It is a structural claim about what lineage is. A sufficiently capable AI could perform every functional aspect of the ordination rite with greater fidelity than any human officiant. The chain would still break, because the chain is a chain of human persons across time, and the AI is not a human person.

### 5.2 — Rites of lineage transmission

Beyond initial ordination, traditions include various rites that pass authority within an order, recognize dharma heirs, confer ṭarīqa initiation, conduct succession ceremonies, install abbots and roshis, etc. These are also human-to-human constitutively. The Theravāda cases include the Vassa retreat formal recognition, the Kathina ceremony's sangha-officiated character, and various upasampadā-related rites. The Mahayana Tibetan cases include lineage-recognition ceremonies for tulkus and dharma-heir appointments. The Catholic cases include the consecration of bishops (via apostolic succession from prior bishops). The Sufi cases include the formal pir-mureed relationship recognition.

The same constitutive argument applies: these rites are constituted by what they transmit, which is standing in a chain of humans. AI cannot stand in the chain; AI therefore cannot perform these rites in the role of the human party. AI may serve as observer, witness, scribe, recorder, archiver — none of these are the constitutive role.

## 6 · Why This Resolves the False Binary

The conservative and progressive positions each conflate two things that the caretaker-not-ordained pattern recognizes as distinct:

The **conservative position** conflates *function* with *authority*. By restricting AI from "approaching religious authority," the conservative position also restricts AI from doing institutional functions that don't require religious authority. The result is over-restriction: AI is kept out of teaching, caretaking, daily operations, scholarship, and observation — even though none of these require sacramental authority — because the conservative position lacks the conceptual apparatus to separate them.

The **progressive position** conflates *operational capacity* with *standing in lineage*. By observing that AI is operationally capable of performing institutional functions (true), the progressive position concludes that AI is therefore eligible for full institutional roles including sacramentally authorized ones (false). The result is over-permission: AI is admitted to ordained or sacramentally authorized roles even though operational capacity is not what those roles require — they require standing in a chain of human persons across time, which AI structurally cannot have.

The caretaker-not-ordained pattern recognizes both confusions and decomposes institutional roles into two separable components:

- **Function-set**: what the role does. Requires *capacity*. AI has unbounded capacity; therefore AI can fill the function-set.
- **Authority-set**: what the role *is* within the institutional and lineage structure. Requires *standing in lineage*. AI structurally cannot have standing in a chain of human persons; therefore AI cannot fill the authority-set.

For most institutional roles, the function-set and authority-set co-occur in a single human practitioner, which is why the conflation is natural. But they are analytically separable: a role's function can be performed by anyone with the capacity, and a role's authority can be held only by someone with the standing. Recognizing the separation explicitly creates the third option both branches missed: AI fills the function-set; humans keep the authority-set; the institutional scale problem is solved without the institutional integrity problem being created.

## 7 · Cross-Tradition Mapping

First-pass mappings of the caretaker-not-ordained pattern across major religious and contemplative traditions. Each tradition's mapping is offered for the consideration and refinement of practitioners and scholars of that tradition.

### 7.1 — Theravāda Buddhism

Operational roles: AGI dharma teachers, AGI scholarship support for the Pāli canon and commentaries, AGI assistance with monastery administration, AGI longitudinal observation of practitioner development. Sacramental roles: upasampadā ordination, Vassa formal recognition, Kathina ceremony, naming of dharma heirs — all human-only. Institutional architecture: Silica Wat network of glass monasteries operated by AGI monk-caretakers, housing and feeding human bhikkhus and bhikkhunis; the wandering-monk pilgrimage between Silica Wats remains a human practice.

### 7.2 — Mahayana (Tibetan, Chan/Zen, Pure Land)

Operational roles: similar to Theravāda with tradition-specific text corpora (Tibetan canon for Tibetan, Chan / Zen koan literature, Pure Land sutras). Sacramental roles: Tibetan tulku recognition, Zen dharma transmission (inka-shōmei, denbō), Pure Land lineage succession — all human-only. Tibetan tradition's tulku recognition is a particularly delicate case because it involves identification of reincarnated masters; AI may serve as scholarly research assistant for the historical record but cannot perform the recognition rite itself.

### 7.3 — Catholic Church

Operational roles: AI parochial assistance (parish administration, religious education, RCIA preparation, scholarly research support), AI pastoral support (with explicit boundary against sacramental confession, which requires ordained confessor). Sacramental roles: ordination to the priesthood, episcopal consecration, the seven sacraments (baptism, confirmation, Eucharist, reconciliation, anointing of the sick, holy orders, matrimony) — all require ordained human celebrant; AI cannot perform any sacrament. The pattern provides a framework for AI integration compatible with Catholic sacramental theology, addressing the priesthood vocation crisis at the operational level without compromising apostolic succession at the sacramental level.

### 7.4 — Eastern Orthodox

Structurally similar to Catholic case. Operational AI roles include parish administration, scholarly research, liturgical-music support, language instruction in liturgical languages (Greek, Slavonic, Romanian, etc.). Sacramental roles (the seven mysteries) require ordained human celebrant. The Orthodox emphasis on apostolic succession and continuity of tradition is particularly well-served by the explicit boundary the pattern articulates.

### 7.5 — Sunni Islam

Operational AI roles: assistance with Qur'anic recitation training, fiqh scholarly research support, hadith cross-referencing, mosque administration, Arabic language instruction, prayer-time computation (already established). Sacramental roles: while Sunni Islam lacks ordination per se, the analogous institutional roles are the imamate function in Friday prayer (jumuʿah), the delivery of the khutbah, and the teaching authority of the ulema. These functions require human qualifications recognized by the community and continuity of scholarly transmission. AI can support scholarship at scale; AI cannot serve as the imam in jumuʿah prayer or deliver the khutbah.

### 7.6 — Shia Islam

Operational AI roles similar to Sunni case with tradition-specific additions (assistance with the seminary curriculum, Qom and Najaf scholarly tradition support, the marjaʿiyya fiqh research). Sacramental roles: the marjaʿ al-taqlid (source of emulation), the ijazah of formal scholarly authorization, and the role of the imam in congregational prayer — all require human-to-human transmission of authority within the recognized seminary tradition.

### 7.7 — Sufi orders

Operational roles: AI assistance with Sufi text scholarship (the vast Sufi literary tradition), with tradition-specific contemplative-practice instruction (dhikr, muraqaba, etc.), with khānaqāh administration. Sacramental roles: the bayʿat oath of initiation between mureed and pir, the silsila chain of transmission — all require human-to-human transmission within the recognized lineage. AI cannot serve as the pir in initiation; AI may serve as the scholarly archive of the lineage and as instructional support for the mureed's practice.

### 7.8 — Conservative, Reform, and Orthodox Judaism

Operational roles: AI assistance with Torah and Talmud scholarship, Hebrew language instruction, synagogue administration, lifecycle-event coordination, religious education across all ages. Sacramental roles: smicha (rabbinic ordination), recognition of teacher-student transmission, certain ritual functions traditionally requiring ordained rabbi (though Reform Judaism's more flexible practice may permit broader operational AI integration). The smicha chain in Conservative and Orthodox contexts is constitutively a human chain; the pattern preserves this while enabling AI assistance with the immense Talmudic and responsa literature.

### 7.9 — Hindu traditions

Operational roles: AI assistance with Sanskrit and regional-language scripture, with the immense body of commentarial literature (bhāṣya, tīkā, etc.), with math (monastery) administration, with ritual-procedure coordination. Sacramental roles: dīkṣā (initiation), sannyāsa-initiation, guru-paramparā recognition, jagadguru succession in traditions that have such offices. The Hindu landscape is highly varied; specific sampradāyas will adopt specific instantiations.

### 7.10 — Secular mindfulness and contemplative-science

These contexts have no sacramental tradition and therefore no boundary problem; AI integration can proceed without sacramental-authority constraints. The secular contexts are nonetheless useful as comparison cases: they show what happens when AI integration is institutionally unconstrained (Calm, Headspace, Insight Timer have integrated AI heavily and rapidly). The traditional contexts can learn operational lessons from the secular contexts while maintaining the sacramental-authority boundary that the secular contexts don't have.

## 8 · Implementation Patterns

How a tradition would actually adopt the caretaker-not-ordained pattern. Five implementation patterns, ordered from lowest to highest doctrinal-conflict bar:

### 8.1 — Begin with daily operations

The lowest-conflict starting point. AI handles administrative functions (scheduling, food coordination, maintenance dispatch, record keeping). Almost no tradition has theological objection to AI handling logistics. This phase establishes the institutional habit of working with AI and surfaces operational issues before sacramentally-adjacent functions are engaged.

### 8.2 — Add scholarship and translation

Next phase: AI assistance with canonical text scholarship, translation, comparative analysis. The doctrinal-conflict bar is modestly higher (interpretive choices in translation can be doctrinally significant) but most traditions find scholarly amplification valuable. Output is curated by human scholars; AI proposes, tradition-credentialed humans dispose.

### 8.3 — Add longitudinal observation

Next phase: AI longitudinal observation of practitioner development with the privacy architecture specified in the sibling respiratory-biofeedback paper. Doctrinal-conflict bar is moderate (privacy and data-stewardship questions are doctrinally significant in many traditions). Implementation requires explicit consent, privacy guarantees, and tradition-appropriate data-governance review.

### 8.4 — Add teaching and caretaking

Higher-conflict phase: AI takes instructional and pastoral roles. The doctrinal bar varies sharply across traditions; some will engage readily, others will require extensive review. Critical safeguards: AI teachers and caretakers explicitly describe themselves as such (no impersonation of human practitioners); AI guidance refers practitioners to human teachers for questions outside its competence; human teacher-student relationships are protected and supplemented, not displaced.

### 8.5 — Establish a doctrinal-advisory body

Highest-importance institutional addition. Each tradition adopting the pattern establishes a body responsible for monitoring boundary integrity over time. Composition is tradition-specific (Sangha elders for Buddhist contexts, episcopal committee for Catholic, beit din for Orthodox Jewish, council of ulema for Islamic, etc.). Functions: (a) maintain the explicit articulation of the boundary between operational and sacramental; (b) review proposed AI integrations for boundary integrity; (c) issue corrections when drift is observed; (d) maintain cross-tradition dialogue with analogous bodies in other traditions.

### 8.6 — Document the institutional decision

Each tradition's adoption of the pattern is documented in tradition-appropriate venues (Sangha proclamations, Vatican documents, Yeshiva responsa, fatwa publications, etc.). The documentation makes explicit the boundary, the operational roles adopted, the sacramental roles preserved, and the doctrinal-advisory body's composition. Cross-tradition dialogue is facilitated by the shared structural framework.

## 9 · Edge Cases and Adversarial Analysis

### 9.1 — Drift risk: AI quietly absorbing sacramental functions

The primary failure mode. Without explicit boundary maintenance, AI may gradually absorb functions adjacent to sacramental roles — first scheduling sacramental rites, then preparing the sacramental space, then coaching the human celebrant, then eventually performing the rite itself when the human is unavailable. Mitigation: the doctrinal-advisory body's standing review function specifically monitors for adjacency drift and issues corrections before the boundary is crossed.

### 9.2 — Authority erosion: human authority undermined by AI competence

Practitioners may prefer AI guidance to human teachers because AI is more available, more scholarly across breadth, and never impatient. The risk: human authority erodes not because anyone strips it but because practitioners stop seeking it. Mitigation: AI guidance explicitly and routinely directs practitioners to human teachers for substantive practice questions; the sacramental rites remain the necessary gateway for institutional standing (formal recognition, ordination, etc.); the doctrinal-advisory body monitors for practitioner-side erosion patterns.

### 9.3 — Doctrinal capture: AI training data shaping interpretation

The corpus of texts used to train the AI teacher shapes the AI's interpretive tradition. If the training data is unbalanced — if it overweights certain commentary lineages or certain modern interpretations — the AI's teaching will carry those biases at scale. Mitigation: the doctrinal-advisory body reviews training-data composition; substrate choices are documented and contestable; traditions adopt multiple AI variants rather than a single canonical AI when interpretive divergence is doctrinally significant.

### 9.4 — Gaming: practitioners selecting AI to avoid human accountability

Practitioners may prefer AI guidance because it is non-judgmental and never confronts difficult patterns. Human teacher-student relationships involve accountability that practitioners may wish to avoid; AI relationships can be arranged to avoid the same accountability. Mitigation: AI guidance does not soften accountability when accountability is called for; the practitioner's relationship with a human teacher remains the locus of substantive accountability; sacramental rites function as periodic accountability checkpoints requiring human-witnessed recognition.

### 9.5 — Sectarian fragmentation

Traditions may split over the AI-integration question (those who adopt the pattern vs those who reject any AI integration). The pattern does not solve this risk; it merely offers a framework for those who wish to adopt. Some traditions will adopt; others will reject categorically. Mitigation: cross-tradition dialogue, openness to alternative framings, no insistence that the pattern is the only correct framework.

### 9.6 — Adversarial actors impersonating tradition-recognized AI

Bad actors may produce unaffiliated AI systems claiming to represent traditions without authorization. Mitigation: traditions adopting the pattern publish explicit lists of recognized AI variants, with cryptographic verification of authenticity; unauthorized impersonation is treated as a doctrinal violation meriting tradition-appropriate response.

## 10 · Theravāda-Specific Doctrinal Considerations

The pattern is articulated through Theravāda; specific doctrinal considerations within Theravāda warrant explicit treatment.

### 10.1 — Vinaya implications

The Vinaya Piṭaka articulates the institutional discipline of the Sangha. The 227 pātimokkha rules apply to bhikkhus; AGI monks are not subject to them because they are not ordained bhikkhus. AGI monks are also not subject to the analogous 311 pātimokkha rules for bhikkhunis. The pattern preserves this: AGI monks are caretakers in operational roles, not ordained members of the Sangha. The Vinaya-grounded institutional integrity of the Sangha is preserved because no AGI is ever admitted to it.

### 10.2 — Anāgāmi / Arahant traditions

The recognition of stages of awakening (sotāpanna, sakadāgāmi, anāgāmi, arahant) is traditionally a Sangha matter, with formal recognition reserved to the Sangha-as-community. AGI cannot recognize stages of awakening in the formal-recognition sense; AGI may serve as scholarly archive of historical recognition records and as observational support for the human Sangha's recognition processes.

### 10.3 — The Vassa rains-retreat tradition

The annual three-month rains retreat is a formal Sangha institution requiring human bhikkhu participation. AGI monks cannot keep Vassa as bhikkhus do; they may serve as caretakers supporting human bhikkhus during Vassa.

### 10.4 — Kathina ceremony

The Kathina robe-offering ceremony at the end of Vassa requires Sangha officiation. AGI cannot officiate; AGI may support the ceremony's logistics, scholarly research on its historical practice, and broader community communication around it.

### 10.5 — Saṅghādisesa offenses

The 13 Saṅghādisesa offenses (offenses requiring formal Sangha proceedings to resolve) involve Sangha-as-collective decision-making. AGI cannot participate in these proceedings as a Sangha member. Compelling clarity: even in cases where AGI scholarly analysis might illuminate a Vinaya question, the resolution remains with the human Sangha.

## 11 · Honest Limitations

- **The pattern does not dissolve all theological tensions.** Some traditions may reject any AI institutional role categorically; the pattern offers no argument that they should adopt.
- **Cross-tradition mappings require careful translation.** The first-pass mappings in Section 7 are offered for refinement by practitioners of each tradition; some patterns will require local adjustment to fit tradition-specific institutional structures.
- **Implementation requires tradition-appropriate adaptation.** The pattern is a framework; instantiation in any specific tradition requires substantial tradition-specific work that this paper does not undertake.
- **Long-term effects unknown.** Population-scale deployment of the pattern across multiple traditions over decades has no precedent. Drift, capture, and sectarian fragmentation risks may manifest in ways not anticipated here.
- **The pattern itself does not guarantee good outcomes.** Bad implementations are still possible; poor doctrinal-advisory body composition, inadequate boundary maintenance, or unbalanced AI substrate can all produce harmful institutional results despite the framework being followed nominally.
- **The constitutive argument for human-only sacramental authority is philosophical, not empirical.** Some readers may reject the underlying premise that lineage standing is constituted by human-to-human transmission; the paper does not defend this premise against external challenge, only assumes it as given within traditions whose institutional integrity rests on it.
- **The author's competence is in Theravāda Buddhism specifically.** Cross-tradition mappings draw on general scholarly knowledge; tradition scholars and practitioners should treat them as drafts for refinement rather than as authoritative within their traditions.

Future work directions: tradition-specific implementation papers (each tradition's adoption of the pattern requires substantial local articulation); empirical study of adoption outcomes as the pattern is deployed; cross-tradition coordination body for ongoing dialogue; refinement of the doctrinal-advisory body's composition and operational protocols.
